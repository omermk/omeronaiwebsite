Why DACH Enterprises Are Stuck at Layer Two

After hundreds of conversations with enterprise leaders, one pattern keeps repeating — and it's not a technology problem.

Omer· May 2026· 6 min read· Share on LinkedIn ↗

The conversation usually goes the same way.

A senior leader — COO, CDO, sometimes the CEO — tells me about their AI programme. The budget is approved. The pilots are running. The demos have impressed the board. They're ahead of some peers, behind others. They are, by any reasonable measure, "doing AI."

And then I ask: how many of those pilots have reached production?

The pause that follows tells me everything.

The layer that matters

Most DACH enterprises are operating at what I call Layer 2: Automating. They have moved past isolated experimentation. AI is doing real work — drafting, summarising, generating code, processing documents. The efficiency gains are genuine.

But the function itself is unchanged. The team structure is the same. The processes are the same. The organisation has learned to use a faster tool. It has not redesigned anything.

Layer 3 — Redesigning — is where that changes. And it requires something that is genuinely uncomfortable: building a second version of the function in parallel.

Why parallel is so hard

The instinct is always to evolve. Take the existing function, introduce AI, improve it incrementally. This feels responsible. It feels manageable. It avoids the political difficulty of telling a team their function might look fundamentally different in two years.

But AI-native functions do not evolve from legacy ones. They operate on different assumptions about what a human adds, what speed is possible, what the error rate should be. Trying to retrofit AI-native logic into a legacy function produces something that is neither — a hybrid that captures the complexity of both and the advantages of neither.

The organisations moving from Layer 2 to Layer 3 are not doing so because they have better technology or bigger budgets. They are doing so because their leaders were willing to hold two competing versions of reality simultaneously, and run the experiment honestly.

What Layer 2 feels like from the inside

The dangerous thing about Layer 2 is that it feels like progress. And it is. The efficiency gains are real. The board is satisfied. The AI programme is visible and funded.

But it creates a subtle organisational trap: the better your Layer 2 results, the harder it becomes to make the argument for Layer 3. Why rebuild something that is already improving?

The answer — and it is one that is hard to make in a board meeting — is that the improving thing will plateau. And by the time that plateau is obvious, the window for redesigning will have narrowed.

The practical question

If you are a DACH enterprise leader reading this, the useful question is not "how do we do more AI?" It is: "which one function, if we built it AI-native from scratch in parallel with our current version, would give us the clearest signal about what we need to rebuild?"

Pick the smallest function where the answer is interesting. Build it in parallel. Run both. Measure honestly. That is what Layer 3 looks like in practice — not a transformation programme, but a controlled experiment with real stakes.
