Perspective

Most DACH enterprises are not adopting AI. They are performing it.

After hundreds of conversations with enterprise leaders across Germany, Austria and Switzerland, one thing is clear: the gap between AI activity and AI impact has never been wider.

Omer · Updated May 2026

The performance trap

The numbers look promising on the surface. Germany sits at 59% AI adoption, behind Europe's 74% average, yes, but moving. Boards are approving budgets. Pilots are launching. Copilot licences are being issued at scale.

But in almost every conversation I have with DACH enterprise leaders, the same pattern emerges: activity without transformation. The pilots run. The demos impress. And then nothing reaches production.

This is not an AI problem. It is a structural one.

The four layers

Layer 1 · Experimenting ~40% of DACH mid-market

Individual Copilot licenses. Isolated pilots. Impressive internal demos. Almost nothing reaching production. The organisation is unchanged.

Layer 2 · Automating ~60% of DACH mid-market

AI applied to bounded tasks: drafting, summarising, coding assistance. Real efficiency gains. But the function itself is structurally unchanged. The organisation runs the same way, just a little faster.

Layer 3 · Redesigning Rare in DACH

Build a new, AI-native version of a function in parallel with the legacy version. AB-test them. Gradually shift. This is where transformation actually begins, and it requires accepting that the two versions are incompatible.

Layer 4 · Augmenting 0% in DACH

External AI agents deployed as actual workforce, working alongside humans. Companies like Bairong in China are already here. No DACH enterprise is close.

Why redesigning is so hard

The uncomfortable truth is this: most organisations run their AI pilots with a hidden assumption baked in: "if we prove this works, we'll integrate it into the existing function later."

That assumption is almost always wrong. Not because people resist change. Because AI-native functions and legacy functions operate on fundamentally different logic. You cannot retrofit one into the other. You have to build in parallel, prove the model, and then make a deliberate decision about which world you want to live in.

The companies that figure this out first will not just be more efficient. They will be structurally different, and structurally superior.

What this means for DACH

Germany's industrial base is not a disadvantage in this transition. It is an asset, if leaders are willing to think about AI not as a productivity tool applied to existing processes, but as an opportunity to redesign those processes entirely.

The window for that decision is narrowing. The companies moving from Layer 2 to Layer 3 today are not doing so because they have better technology. They are doing so because they have leaders willing to hold two versions of their function simultaneously, and the patience to run the experiment properly.
